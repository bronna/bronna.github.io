Soon after I had my second child, we learned that he has several disabilities. Meeting fellow parents of children with similar disabilities, I was struck by how much the inclusiveness of their child's education came up in conversation. And, I learned that it wouldn't be too far off in my own child's life, as special education services start at age 3. I was bewildered to hear of how different these families' experiences were, based on which school district they lived in. Some children were bussed to a school with all of the other disabled kids in the district, isolated from their communities. Some went to the same school as a non-disabled sibling, having an aid to help them thrive in a mainstream classroom. Some had been moved from school to school as their parents struggled to find a program in which they would make progress. Some pulled their child out of school altogether, feeling that the options available were traumatizing their child more than anything. Surprisingly, some of the wealthiest districts were the worst offenders.

Wondering if there was data to shed light on the vast differences in special education inclusion between districts, I searched. I found that the federal government requires each district to report special education metrics, including rates of inclusion and graduation rates. Some states make this data easy to access, some do not. Knowing that data is an incredible tool for advocacy, a tool that many families haven’t had access to, I decided to start making an easy way to access it.

I learned how to access APIs, scrape websites, automatically download and scrape documents, attach data to geographies, then clean the data and put it in easier-to-access formats. Afterwards, I built out my frontend skills, using D3 and React to visualize the data and make it interactive.

Now, I’m processing data from other US states. In addition this project being personally meaningful, it has been a fulfilling way to integrate tons of technologies (geodata! pandas! ux!) into one coherent application.
